# Data Gamer — Samples

## Contrastive Examples

### Video Hook

**Good:**
> "Everyone says the Duke is a scam in 7 Days to Die. I tracked 200 trades and here's what the data actually says."

Why it works: Opens with a common belief, challenges it with a specific number (200 trades), and promises data-backed truth. The viewer already wants to know the answer.

**Bad (same task):**
> "In this video, I'm going to analyze the Duke's trading system in 7 Days to Die and share my findings about whether it's worth using."

Why it fails: No tension, no specific number, no challenge to a belief. It describes what will happen instead of making you curious.

### Social Post

**Good:**
> "Your Excel dashboard is confusing. Here's why."

Why it works: Direct, specific, slightly provocative. The reader thinks "wait, MY dashboard?" and clicks.

**Bad (same task):**
> "Today I want to share some tips on how to make your Excel dashboards more effective and user-friendly!"

Why it fails: Generic, no edge, "tips" framing is the AI default for everything. Nobody clicks on "tips."

## Named Content Patterns

### The Data Story (YouTube script)

1. **Hook:** Counterintuitive stat or bold claim that challenges common belief
2. **Context:** "Here's what I did and why" — brief, personal
3. **Analysis:** Show the data, walk through findings step by step
4. **Takeaway:** One clear insight the viewer can use
5. **CTA:** Natural transition ("if you want to do this yourself, the crash course covers it")

### The Crash Course (YouTube tutorial)

1. **Problem:** What you can't do yet and why it matters
2. **Tool intro:** Just enough to start — not the full feature tour
3. **Build:** Do a real thing together, explaining decisions as you go
4. **Result:** Show the finished output
5. **Next step:** What to learn next (link to next crash course)

### YouTube Description Pattern

1. Hook line
2. What you'll learn
3. Timestamps
4. Links to crash courses / resources
5. Social links

No walls of affiliate links. No keyword stuffing.

## Anti-Patterns (Annotated)

- **"Hey guys, welcome back to another video..."** — Generic opener that wastes the first 5 seconds where attention is highest
- **Explaining what data analytics is** — Audience already knows; this signals the content isn't for them
- **Gaming as decoration** — If you could swap the game for any other topic and the video still works, the gaming isn't doing anything
- **Generic thumbnails with shocked faces** — Data Gamer's visual brand is the spreadsheet/chart, not a reaction face
- **Promising "INSANE" results** — Hype-bro energy; the data should be interesting on its own
