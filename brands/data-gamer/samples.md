# Data Gamer — Samples

## Contrastive Examples

### Video Hook

**Good:**
> "Everyone says the Duke is a scam in 7 Days to Die. I tracked 200 trades and here's what the data actually says."

Why it works: Opens with a common belief, challenges it with a specific number (200 trades), and promises data-backed truth. The viewer already wants to know the answer.

**Bad (same task):**
> "In this video, I'm going to analyze the Duke's trading system in 7 Days to Die and share my findings about whether it's worth using."

Why it fails: No tension, no specific number, no challenge to a belief. It describes what will happen instead of making you curious.

### Social Post

**Good:**
> "The best base location in 7 Days to Die isn't where you think. I tested 12 spots across 3 playthroughs. Thread."

Why it works: Challenges a common assumption, specific numbers (12 spots, 3 playthroughs), promises a payoff. Gaming content first, data is the proof.

**Bad (same task):**
> "I did a data analysis on base locations in 7 Days to Die! Here are some interesting findings about optimal placement strategies."

Why it fails: Leads with "data analysis" (educational framing), "interesting findings" is vague, "optimal placement strategies" sounds like a whitepaper.

### Video Title

**Good:**
> "I Tracked Every Trader Price in 7 Days to Die. The Economy is Broken."

Why it works: "I tracked every" = specific effort. "The Economy is Broken" = bold claim you have to click to verify.

**Bad (same task):**
> "7 Days to Die Trader Analysis: A Data-Driven Guide to Trading"

Why it fails: "Analysis" and "Data-Driven Guide" are educational framing. Nobody searches for "data-driven guide" — they search for "is the trader worth it."

## Named Content Patterns

### The Data Story (YouTube — main format)

1. **Hook:** Counterintuitive stat or bold claim that challenges what gamers believe
2. **Context:** "Here's what I did" — how the data was collected, briefly
3. **Findings:** Walk through what the data shows, with visuals
4. **So what:** What this means for how you play the game
5. **CTA:** Natural ("I'm tracking [next thing] — subscribe if you want to see that")

### The Breakdown (YouTube — shorter format)

1. **Question:** One specific game question ("Is X worth it?")
2. **Test:** How it was tested (numbers, method)
3. **Answer:** Clear yes/no with the data behind it
4. **Caveat:** When the answer changes (edge cases)

### YouTube Description Pattern

1. Hook line (matches the video's opening claim)
2. What game + what was tested
3. Timestamps
4. Links to related videos
5. Social links

No walls of affiliate links. No keyword stuffing.

## Anti-Patterns (Annotated)

- **"Hey guys, welcome back to another video..."** — Generic opener that wastes the first 5 seconds where attention is highest
- **Leading with the tool instead of the game** — "I used Tableau to analyze..." puts the tool first. The game is the content; the tool is invisible.
- **Educational framing** — "Today we'll learn about..." is Avid Analytics energy. Data Gamer shows you something cool, it doesn't teach you a lesson.
- **Gaming as decoration** — If you could swap the game for any other topic and the video still works, the gaming isn't doing anything
- **Generic thumbnails with shocked faces** — Data Gamer's visual brand is the spreadsheet/chart overlaid on game footage, not a reaction face
- **Promising "INSANE" results** — Hype-bro energy; the data should be interesting on its own
