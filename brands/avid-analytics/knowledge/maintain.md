# Maintain

> What keeps it running? Money, tools, legal, systems, people, admin.

## Infrastructure

Start simple and invest only when complexity is earned. Prioritize what's customer-facing first — your website, payment system, and communication tools matter more than your internal stack. Don't over-build before you've validated demand; a laptop, a domain, and a payment processor is enough to start most businesses. Upgrade when the current setup starts costing you time, reliability, or sales — not before.

## Systems

Build systems that work hard for you — consistent, measurable, and repeatable. A good system runs whether your willpower shows up or not, and when every process is documented and trackable, you can make adjustments and immediately see whether things got better or worse. The endgame is a business that runs without you. That means removing yourself from low-value work through AI, automation, and a lean team built around outcomes rather than headcount.

As you free up time, your role shifts from operator to architect — installing leaders who own results, building a brand that doesn't depend on you being in the room, and focusing on the decisions that actually move the business forward. Eventually, that freed-up time and capital lets you do what you do best: solve new problems, launch new ventures, and multiply what's already working. Anchor it all to a set of north star metrics — the numbers that tell you whether the business is healthy and growing.

**What to Systemize First**

You can't systemize everything at once, and automating the wrong thing first wastes effort. Use this simple lens to prioritize: score each candidate process on three factors, then start with the highest total.

| Factor | Question to ask | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | How often does this happen? | Daily = 3, Weekly = 2, Monthly = 1 |
| **Time cost** | How long does it take each time? | 1+ hour = 3, 15–60 min = 2, Under 15 min = 1 |
| **Error risk** | How often does it go wrong or produce inconsistent results? | Often = 3, Sometimes = 2, Rarely = 1 |

A process that scores 7–9 is your first priority. A process that scores 3–4 can wait. When two processes score similarly, pick the one that's closest to revenue — customer-facing and delivery processes before internal admin.

Common first wins (in rough order):
1. **Client onboarding** — high frequency, high error risk, directly affects first impressions
2. **Invoicing & follow-up** — recurring, easy to automate, directly tied to cash flow
3. **Lead follow-up sequences** — high frequency, high cost of dropping the ball, easy to templatize
4. **Content repurposing & distribution** — time-heavy, repeatable, low error risk (good automation candidate)
5. **Reporting & metrics** — time-heavy when manual, easy to automate with dashboards

Don't systemize things you should eliminate. Before building a process around something, ask: "Does this actually need to happen?" If the answer is no, delete it. The best system is the one you never had to build.

**Building Systems**

Building systems and documenting them. All to make business knowledge. Repeatability & Reproducibility. Simplify as much as possible to make it easier for AI and people to manage and execute.

- **Workflows:** SOPs (Clear textual with typography and clear visuals/diagrams to make things even more clear). Use process improvement to make these better over time and find out where to use AI tools/agents to further automate. Every SOP's scope should be clearly defined.
- **Mapping:** Systems/Infrastructure (Diagrams, UMLs, etc), Kanban boards, any visual representation to make the process easier to see at a high level.
- **Business Knowledge:** Technical documentation, business glossary, infographics, cheat sheets.
- **Automation** always boils down to one pattern: something happens → something runs. It's not about replacing people — it's about freeing them to focus on what matters most.
  - **Trigger:** The event, signal, or condition that tells the system to act — a new order, a scheduled time, a threshold crossed, a form submitted.
  - **Workflow:** The work the system performs in response.
    - *Static:* Fixed scripts, rules, and flow logic that execute the same way every time. Predictable, reliable, easy to debug.
    - *Dynamic:* AI agents that adapt based on context to complete an objective. Could be things like…
      - An Executive assistant to help manage an Inbox & Calendar, appointment setting, or scheduling coordination
      - Sales bot (Atlas) to lead capture + sequences + CRM workflows to contact leads, qualify them, sell, and book meetings—assuming you have a reliable source of leads to start from.
  - **Orchestration:** Stringing multiple automations together so the output of one becomes the trigger for the next. Individual automations handle tasks; orchestration handles processes. This is how simple automations scale into systems that run entire workflows end to end.
- **Great teams:** People who tackle lower value things for you.
- **Scalability:** Systems create room for growth without multiplying effort.
- **Modularity:** Change out what we need. Component-based design is what leads this the most so that we can mix and match how we wish.
- **Repeatability tools** for consistency:
  - *Practical Defaults:* Whenever feasible, auto-select the most common or optimal settings so the process "just works" without extra setup.
  - *Templates:* Provide reusable patterns that reduce guesswork, cut down setup time, and make processes predictable. AI is amazing at filling out templates.
  - *Presets:* Offer ready-to-use configurations tailored to different scenarios. Easy preset switching allows flexibility without the hassle of changeovers or needing deep expertise.
- **Continuous improvements:** Allow easy feedback loops for continuous improvement. Compare against its own baseline as well as standardized benchmarks.

## People

Departments need to have leaders who own outcomes.

To scale your business, you need to get people in. Delegate low value tasks first to buy back your time. The goal is to use money to buy back your time. But it's not about getting huge teams, all you need is a few talented people. Like 10 people or less.

- **When to hire:** When a task is repeatable, documented, and eating time you should spend on growth or strategy. If you're doing $10/hour work instead of $100/hour thinking, that's the signal.
- **Who first:** Start with what drains you most. Usually: admin/ops, then delivery support, then sales support. Hire for the role, not the résumé — look for people who are reliable, resourceful, and can own outcomes without micromanagement.
- **How to manage:** Set clear expectations, measure by outcomes (not hours), give feedback regularly, and get out of their way. The goal is people who make decisions you'd make — so invest in alignment upfront.

## Finance

Breakeven analysis, profit margins, cashflow management. What matters is what you take home.

Struggling to make great margins or grow? Interview your clients and ask "What did you pay for from us that created the most value?" Then you focus on that, turning that into a productized service.

**Pricing Strategy**

Price based on the value you create, not the time you spend. The customer doesn't care how long it took — they care what it's worth to them. If your solution saves someone $50,000/year, a $5,000 price feels like a bargain regardless of whether it took you 2 hours or 200.

- **Understand their cost of inaction:** During interviews and sales conversations, quantify what the problem costs them — in money, time, stress, missed opportunities. This number is your pricing ceiling. Your price should feel small relative to the pain it removes.
- **Anchor to outcomes, not inputs:** "This will save you 10 hours a week" justifies a higher price than "this takes me 3 hours to build." Frame the price against the result, not your effort.
- **Test price points, not just demand:** During validation, don't just ask "would you pay for this?" — ask "would you pay $X for this?" Test at least two price points across different conversations. If everyone says yes instantly, you're probably too cheap. If everyone hesitates, you're either too expensive or the value isn't clear enough.
- **Use your 3-tier structure to learn:** Your low/middle/high tiers aren't just a sales tactic — they're a pricing experiment. Track which tier people choose. If most pick low, your middle tier's value gap isn't clear. If most pick high, your middle is underpriced.
- **Raise prices when demand allows it:** If you're closing more than 80% of proposals, your prices are too low. Raise by 10-20%, see what happens. The right price is where you close 40-60% of qualified prospects — enough to grow, selective enough to maintain quality.

**Protect your margins**

Aim to have margin be at least 80%, ideally 90%+.

1. Set your price floor: Cost × 5 (this ensures room for overhead, reinvestment, and profit — anything less and you're trading time for money)
2. Productize your offer (Ask AI to help with this)
3. Scope the outcome (Set limits, don't let people enable scope creep)
4. Monitor the margins — review monthly and flag anything below 70% for immediate action

**Cash flow discipline**

Revenue doesn't matter if you can't cover next month's expenses. Track money in vs. money out weekly, keep a minimum runway buffer (3–6 months of expenses), and separate business money from personal money from day one.

**When to reinvest**

Reinvest in what moves the needle — marketing that's working, systems that save time, people who free you up. Don't reinvest in "nice to have" until the core is profitable and stable.

**Know your numbers**

Track 3 numbers weekly: revenue, expenses, and profit margin. Everything else is detail. If you can't explain where your money went last month in 30 seconds, your tracking needs work.

## Legal & Administration

This isn't the exciting part, but it's the part that protects everything else. Get the basics in place early so you're not scrambling when something goes wrong or when you're ready to scale. You don't need a lawyer on retainer from day one — you need to make a few smart decisions upfront and know when to get professional help.

- **Business formation:** At minimum, separate yourself from the business legally. An LLC is the default for most small businesses — it's cheap, simple, and protects your personal assets if things go sideways. Sole prop is fine for testing an idea, but once money is flowing, form the entity. Talk to an accountant about the tax implications before choosing (LLC vs S-corp election matters for how you pay yourself).
- **Contracts:** Always have one. Even with friends, even for small projects. A good contract covers: what you're delivering, what it costs, the timeline, what happens if scope changes, payment terms, and how either side can walk away. You can start with templates, but get a lawyer to review your standard agreement once you're using it regularly.
- **IP protection:** If your brand name matters to your business, trademark it. If you create original content, courses, or software, understand basic copyright. You don't need to file patents on day one, but know what's yours and take simple steps to protect it (watermarks, terms of use, copyright notices).
- **Insurance:** General liability insurance is cheap and covers you if something goes wrong with a client or customer. Professional liability (errors & omissions) matters if you're giving advice or delivering services. Get quotes early — it's usually less expensive than people assume.
- **Compliance:** Register your business with your state/city, get any required licenses or permits, and set up proper tax registration (sales tax, EIN, etc.). These vary by location and industry — 30 minutes of research now saves major headaches later.

## Continuous Improvement

**Purpose:** Make the business a little better every week.

Operations will never be perfect on the first pass—or the tenth. The goal isn't perfection; it's building the habit of noticing what's not working and fixing it. Small improvements compound. A business that gets 1% better each week is unrecognizable in a year. One that "gets to it later" stays stuck.

Always work around theory of constraints. Eliminate time and complexity: Simplify as much as possible and then automate with scripts/logic/rules and AI agents. But also don't just eliminate steps (Find ways to add value/make more money), build copilots/AI assistants – these tools run all the time consistently, where we can't do so.

**Magnitudes**

1. **Feedback Loops:** Small, immediate, reversible adjustments made during normal work to smooth friction, correct issues, and keep the system healthy. Uses quick observation rather than formal measurement.
2. **Experimentation:** Structured tests where you try a meaningful change with a clear hypothesis and 1–2 simple metrics to see if it performs better than the current standard. Short duration, reversible, and used when an idea is big enough to test intentionally.
3. **Projects:** Larger, high-magnitude improvement efforts that require deeper analysis, cross-team coordination, or system redesign. Uses formal methods (like DMAIC) when complexity or ROI is high, leading to lasting, transformational changes.

**Error Proofing**

Prevent the error → Assist correct execution → Detect issues early.

- **Prevention:** Can we redesign this so the mistake can't happen? The goal is to make the error physically or logically impossible — so you can't mess it up. Think of a microwave that won't run with the door open, an ATM that returns your card before giving cash, or a web form that won't submit until all required fields are filled correctly.
- **Facilitation:** If not, ask: Can we make it easier to do correctly? It's designing systems, tools, and environments so intuitive that doing the right thing becomes the easiest (even default) path. The goal is to make correct execution feel natural, while mistakes become physically or logically difficult to make. In other words: make success effortless and failure inconvenient.
- **Detection:** If that's still not possible, ensure there's a fast, reliable way to catch it. You'll know immediately if something went wrong. Use systems like spellcheck in a word processor or smoke alarms that alert you as soon as an issue occurs — so errors are quickly noticed and able to be corrected before causing harm.

Control systems and feedback loops to help maintain quality standards and encourage continuous improvement.
