# Fulfill

> What do we do? Delivering the promise.

**Deliver With A System (Operations)**

Simple, easy, and repeatable.

1. **Kickoff call:** Doing this after a purchase makes everyone feel good and aligns expectations.
2. **Gather inputs:** Get what you need ahead of time to be successful. Don't gather it as you're going along.
3. **Get it built:** Ship the deliverable, even a small quick win (Ideally within 5 days, but commit to 7 so you under-commit and over-deliver).
4. **Review:** Review how you're moving your clients forward (Kanban?).
5. **Get the win:** What's the time to first value? Then ask the client if they know anyone else like them they could recommend for us to help as well — Referrals. "We agree you've been getting great results so far, I appreciate as a great client to work with… I have a question. Who are one or two people, or anyone you know who would be a good fit for this program as well?" (Not asking "Do you have any", but a more affirmative "Who are some people that would…")

## Communication

Clients respect availability when it's treated like a professional operating rule, not a personal preference. That means: you set expectations early, route work through a process, make off-hours access impossible by default, and enforce the rule the same way every time.

**What to do (in order)**

- **Expectations:** Set business hours + response time (ex: "Mon–Fri, 10–4 ET, replies within 1 business day"). Add: off-hours messages are received, not acted on, until next business window.
- **One lane for work:** Pick 1–2 channels (email + task board). Everything else gets redirected. Avoid giving a direct phone number unless you want true access.
- **Calendar control:** Use a booking link that only shows your available slots; calls happen only via the link (no spontaneous or off-hours calls).
- **Queue the work:** "New requests go into the backlog; I'll confirm ETA." This stops "do it now" energy.
- **Define urgent:** Agree what counts as urgent and what doesn't. If they want after-hours, offer a rush option you must explicitly accept (and charge for). No confirmation = it waits.
- **Enforce gently but consistently:** Don't "reward" off-hours messages/calls with immediate work. Either wait, or send one line acknowledging + when you'll handle it. If they call at midnight, don't answer—redirect next day.

**Copy/paste responses**

- **After-hours message:** "Got this—I'm offline now. I'll pick it up tomorrow and update you by 11am ET."
- **Midnight call / off-hours call:** "I saw your call. I take calls Mon–Fri, 10–4 ET via my calendar: [link]."
- **Random call ask:** "I can do a call—please grab a slot on my calendar here [link]."
- **New request:** "Happy to do it. Can you add it to the task board? I'll confirm ETA."
- **"Urgent" push:** "If this needs tonight, I can do a rush turnaround at [rate], pending my confirmation. Want me to proceed?"

If they still ignore it: step up the firmness without drama: reminder → remove/limit direct phone access + require booking link → require requests through the process → charge rush → end the engagement if it's chronic.

**When to Say No (Disqualifying & Firing Clients)**

Not every customer is a good customer. A bad-fit client costs more than an empty slot — they drain your time, erode your margins, demoralize your team, and distract you from the clients who actually value what you do. Learning to say no early (and exit gracefully when you didn't) is a business skill, not a luxury.

*Red flags before signing:*

- **Scope creep in the pitch:** If they're already asking for extras, custom exceptions, or "just one more thing" before they've paid, it will only get worse after. This signals they don't respect boundaries — and your margins will suffer.
- **Price grinding without value recognition:** Negotiating is normal. But if the entire conversation is about getting a lower price and never about the outcome, they don't value the transformation — they see you as a commodity. These clients will never be satisfied at any price.
- **Disrespect for your process:** Ignoring your booking link, calling outside hours during the sales process, pressuring for immediate responses. How someone behaves before they pay is the best-case version of how they'll behave after.
- **Misaligned expectations:** They want something fundamentally different from what you offer, and you'd have to bend your model to fit. If delivering for them means becoming a different business, the answer is no.
- **Gut feeling:** If something feels off — vague about what they want, evasive about budget, bad-mouthing every previous provider — trust that. You don't need a bullet-point reason to decline.

*How to decline gracefully:*

- "I appreciate you reaching out. Based on what you've described, I don't think I'm the best fit for this — but I'd recommend [alternative] who specializes in that."
- "I want to be upfront — this project is outside the scope of what I do well, and I'd rather tell you now than deliver something that doesn't meet your expectations."

*When to fire an existing client:*

- They consistently ignore boundaries despite repeated, escalating enforcement.
- The engagement is unprofitable after accounting for the time, stress, and opportunity cost.
- They treat you or your team disrespectfully.
- They demand work outside the agreed scope and refuse to pay for it.
- Keeping them prevents you from taking on better-fit, higher-value work.

*How to exit:*

- Finish any committed deliverables or reach a clean stopping point.
- Be direct but professional: "I've thought about this carefully, and I don't think I'm the right fit for where you're headed. I want to wrap up our current work cleanly and help you transition to someone who's a better match."
- Don't negotiate. This isn't an objection to overcome — it's a decision. Be kind, be firm, be done.
- Offer a referral if genuine. Don't burn the bridge — just stop walking across it.

The math is simple: one bad client can consume the time and energy of three good ones. Saying no to the wrong people is how you make room for the right ones.
