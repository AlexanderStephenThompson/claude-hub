# Avid Analytics — Samples

## Contrastive Examples

### Client-Facing Recommendation

**Good:**
> "There are three options here. Custom gives you flexibility but doubles your timeline. I'd go Tableau."

Why it works: Names options, names the tradeoff, gives the recommendation. The client knows exactly what you think and why.

**Bad (same task):**
> "After evaluating several potential solutions, we believe there may be value in exploring Tableau as a possible option for your analytics needs."

Why it fails: Hedging ("may be value," "possible option"), corporate distance ("we believe"), no tradeoff named. The client learns nothing.

### LinkedIn Post

**Good:**
> "In my experience, businesses at your stage get the most ROI from fixing their file structure before touching their dashboards. You can't visualize data you can't find."

Why it works: Grounds in experience, gives a specific non-obvious recommendation, ends with a memorable line.

**Bad (same task):**
> "Data organization is a key component of any successful analytics strategy. Companies should prioritize building a strong data foundation before investing in visualization tools."

Why it fails: Generic truism, no personal voice, "key component" and "strong foundation" are empty phrases. Could be anyone's post.

### Proposal Opening

**Good:**
> "Your team spends 4 hours a week looking for files that should take 30 seconds to find. This proposal fixes that."

Why it works: Specific pain point with a number, direct promise. Client sees their problem reflected back.

**Bad (same task):**
> "Thank you for the opportunity to submit this proposal. Avid Analytics is a data consultancy specializing in helping organizations optimize their information management processes."

Why it fails: Thank-you opener wastes the highest-attention position. Describes the company instead of the client's problem.

## Named Content Patterns

### The Proposal

1. State the problem concisely (with a specific metric if possible)
2. Name the deliverables with scope caps
3. Explain what success looks like
4. Price it

No fluff. No "about us" section at the top.

### The Tier Narrative (for service pages)

1. Name the pain ("chronic 'where is that file?' question")
2. Name the transformation ("your team stops wasting mental energy on navigation")
3. Name the result ("because it was designed around how they actually work")

### The LinkedIn Insight Post

1. Grounded claim ("In my experience...")
2. Non-obvious recommendation
3. One-line takeaway that's quotable

## Anti-Patterns (Annotated)

- **Proposals without scope caps** — Invites scope creep; every Avid Analytics deliverable has defined boundaries
- **Hedging language** — "Maybe we could" or "I think it could work" undermines the consultant authority that clients are paying for
- **Jargon without explanation** — One industry term per sentence max; if you need two, explain one
- **Presenting options without a recommendation** — The client hired you to think; don't hand them a menu
- **Using "we" for a solo practice** — Use "I" — it's more honest and more authoritative
