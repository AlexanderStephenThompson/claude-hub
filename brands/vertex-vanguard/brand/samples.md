# Vertex Vanguard — Samples

## Contrastive Examples

### Tutorial Opening

**Good:**
> "Every time I exported from Blender to Unity, something broke. Scale was off, normals were flipped, or the whole thing rotated 90 degrees. Here's the settings that finally fixed it."

Why it works: Opens with a relatable frustration, lists specific symptoms (not vague "problems"), promises a concrete fix. The reader thinks "that happened to me too."

**Bad (same task):**
> "In this comprehensive tutorial, we will explore the intricacies of Blender's FBX export pipeline and learn how to properly configure export settings for Unity integration."

Why it fails: "Comprehensive tutorial" and "intricacies" are academic framing. "We will explore" is passive. No personal experience, no specific problem. Could be any tutorial site.

### Framework Explanation

**Good:**
> "A good VRChat world is really just: a spawn space, social zones at different scales, and connective tissue between them. Everything else is a feature layer."

Why it works: Takes something complex (world design) and reduces it to a clean, memorable framework. "Really just" signals "this is simpler than you think."

**Bad (same task):**
> "VRChat world design involves several key components including spawn areas, social spaces of various sizes, transitional corridors, and additional interactive elements that enhance the user experience."

Why it fails: Lists the same concepts but without the framework lens. "Key components" and "enhance the user experience" are generic filler. Nothing memorable or reusable.

### Design Principle

**Good:**
> "The worlds people remember aren't necessarily the most complex; they're the ones with the strongest mood. A lo-fi rain world with great audio can outperform a technically impressive one with no soul."

Why it works: Counterintuitive claim backed by a specific example. Gives permission to prioritize mood over technical complexity.

**Bad (same task):**
> "When designing VRChat worlds, it's important to focus on creating an immersive atmosphere rather than just adding technical features."

Why it fails: Same insight, stripped of all personality. "It's important to" is throat-clearing. No specific example to make it memorable.

## Named Content Patterns

### The Problem-Fix Tutorial

1. **Problem:** What goes wrong (the specific pain point)
2. **Context:** Why it happens (brief, 1-2 sentences)
3. **Solution:** Step-by-step with exact settings/numbers
4. **Why it works:** The principle behind the fix
5. **Reuse:** How to apply this to other situations

### The Framework Distillation

1. Name the complex topic
2. "It's really just [simple framework]"
3. Walk through each piece with an example
4. Show how the framework applies to a real project

### The Behind-the-Scenes (YouTube)

1. Show the current state of a project
2. Name what you're working on and why
3. Show the messy process (failures included)
4. End with what you learned

## Anti-Patterns (Annotated)

- **Starting with theory** — VV teaches by doing; open with the problem, not the concept
- **"Step 1: Open Blender"** — Audience already knows the basics; skip to the non-obvious part
- **Treating VRChat as a game** — It's a creative platform; "playing VRChat" vs "building in VRChat" are different audiences
- **Showing only the polished result** — The process (including failures) is the content
- **Tutorials without reusable principles** — Every fix should teach a pattern, not just a solution
