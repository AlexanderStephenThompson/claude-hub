# Crop & Chop — Samples

## Contrastive Examples

### Email Subject

**Good:**
> "Your greens are ready"

Why it works: Short (4 words), personal ("your"), feels like a text from a friend. No urgency, no hype.

**Bad (same task):**
> "This Week's Fresh Harvest Is Here — Don't Miss Out!"

Why it fails: Title case screams marketing, "don't miss out" is urgency/scarcity language, sounds like a newsletter blast.

### Product Description

**Good:**
> "Sunflower Microgreens — Nutty, crunchy, and surprisingly filling. These are the gateway green — even microgreen skeptics love them. Great on salads, sandwiches, or straight out of the container (we won't judge)."

Why it works: Three sensory words in the first sentence, "gateway green" is a memorable phrase, parenthetical adds personality, no health claims.

**Bad (same task):**
> "Our sunflower microgreens are harvested at peak freshness and contain high levels of vitamins E, B complex, and essential amino acids. A nutritious addition to any meal."

Why it fails: Leads with harvest process (nobody cares), pivots to nutrient list (supplement label energy), "nutritious addition" is clinical and generic.

### Social Post

**Good:**
> "This week's harvest: pea shoots, sunflower, and radish. The radish has this peppery kick that's perfect on tacos. Subscribers: yours are packed and ready. Everyone else: see you Saturday at the market!"

Why it works: Specific varieties, sensory detail ("peppery kick"), speaks to both audiences (subscribers + market shoppers), warm sign-off.

**Bad (same task):**
> "New microgreens available! Our fresh, locally-grown greens are packed with nutrients. Visit us at the farmers market or subscribe for weekly delivery. Link in bio!"

Why it fails: "Locally-grown" and "packed with nutrients" are banned phrases, generic structure, "link in bio" is lazy, no sensory detail.

## Named Content Patterns

### The Weekly Harvest Post (Social)

1. What's in this week's harvest (name specific varieties)
2. One sensory detail about a standout variety
3. Subscriber acknowledgment
4. Market shoppers invitation

### The Product Card (Website)

1. Variety name
2. Three sensory words (taste + texture + one surprise)
3. One memorable phrase ("the gateway green")
4. Usage suggestion with personality

### The Laura Post (Mascot Social)

1. Laura speaks in first person
2. One thing she's excited about this week
3. Warm, enthusiastic, never salesy
4. Under 30 words

## Anti-Patterns (Annotated)

- **Health claims as selling points** — Legally risky and tonally wrong; sell the flavor, not the vitamins
- **Urgency/scarcity language** — "Limited supply! Order NOW!" is the opposite of neighborly
- **Emoji walls** — More than 2 emoji per post makes it look like a MLM pitch
- **Generic food photography advice** — "Flat lay with marble background" is Instagram 2018; be authentic instead
- **Corporate "about us" language** — This is a local farm brand, not a Fortune 500 company page
