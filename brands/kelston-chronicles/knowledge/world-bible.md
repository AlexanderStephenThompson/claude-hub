# Kelston Chronicles — World Bible

The canonical world reference for the Kelston Chronicles. This is the single source of truth for world consistency. The **world-writer** agent uses this to expand content. The **consistency-checker** agent uses this to validate content.

This is a living document. Sections marked **TBD** are intentionally unresolved — agents must not fill them in or make assumptions about them.

---

## Core Concept

**Resident-adapted architecture.**

Kelston is a world where the built environment is designed from the ground up for inhabitants with fundamentally different physical forms. This is not accessibility retrofitted onto a standard design — it is architecture that assumes diversity of body type, size, and lifestyle as the baseline.

A doorway in Kelston is not "one size fits all with a ramp added." It is designed from the start to serve whoever lives behind it. A market stall is built differently depending on whether its regulars are small enough to perch on a countertop or tall enough to browse from standing height. The furniture, the signage, the ceiling height, the width of the streets — every physical detail reflects who lives there.

This is the creative backbone of the entire world. Every region, location, and resident should reinforce this idea: architecture adapts to residents, not the other way around.

## Regions Reference

Eight regions, each with a distinct biome, mood, and visual identity. Regions blend at their borders — there are no hard edges between biomes.

### Tundra

- **Biome:** Arctic / subarctic tundra
- **Color palette:** Cool blues, whites, silver-grey
- **Mood:** Quiet, vast, still
- **Architecture notes:** Insulated, low-profile structures that retain heat. Heavy walls, small windows, warm interiors that contrast the cold exterior. Buildings designed for residents with thick fur or dense builds — wide corridors, reinforced flooring, heated stone. Entrances face away from prevailing winds.

### Desert

- **Biome:** Arid desert / dry steppe
- **Color palette:** Warm golds, sandstone, burnt orange
- **Mood:** Bright, expansive, dry heat
- **Architecture notes:** Thick-walled structures for thermal mass — cool interiors during the day, retained warmth at night. Covered walkways, shaded courtyards, wind towers for ventilation. Architecture accounts for residents who burrow, who need shade canopies, or whose body types handle heat differently. Water infrastructure is central.

- **Known residents:** TBD

### Rainforest

- **Biome:** Tropical / subtropical rainforest
- **Color palette:** Deep greens, mossy tones, dappled light
- **Mood:** Humid, layered, alive
- **Architecture notes:** Vertical layers — canopy-level walkways, mid-level platforms, ground-level paths. Open-air construction with drainage built into everything. Architecture designed for climbers (grip surfaces, branch-like handholds), flyers (launch/landing platforms at height), and ground-dwellers (covered root-level paths). Living walls, integrated vegetation.

- **Known residents:** TBD

### Coastal

- **Biome:** Temperate / subtropical coastline
- **Color palette:** Salt-washed blues, driftwood grey, seafoam
- **Mood:** Breezy, tidal, weathered
- **Architecture notes:** Weather-resistant materials — salt-treated wood, stone foundations. Buildings account for tidal changes and storm winds. Architecture serves aquatic and semi-aquatic residents alongside land-dwellers — water-access ramps, partially submerged structures, drying areas. Piers and docks are load-bearing for varied body sizes.

### Alpine

- **Biome:** High-altitude mountain
- **Color palette:** Crisp whites, slate grey, ice blue
- **Mood:** Elevated, thin air, sharp light
- **Architecture notes:** Built into mountainsides and ridgelines. Steep-access paths with switchbacks for ground-dwellers and open ledges for flyers or climbers. Structures anchored against wind and snow load. Terraced construction. Interior spaces compensate for thin air — lower ceilings conserve warmth. Views are dramatic and deliberate.

### Wetlands

- **Biome:** Freshwater marshland / swamp
- **Color palette:** Muted greens, brown-grey, still water reflection
- **Mood:** Still, reflective, patient
- **Architecture notes:** Raised platforms and stilt construction. Boardwalk networks connect structures over water. Architecture serves amphibious residents (water-to-land transitions built in), wading residents (shallow-water paths), and dry-preference residents (elevated, moisture-sealed spaces). Wood and reed construction. Everything is designed to coexist with water levels that shift.

- **Known residents:** TBD

### Urban Canopy

- **Biome:** Dense, vertical urban environment built into/around massive trees or structures
- **Color palette:** Warm amber, lamplight gold, deep wood tones
- **Mood:** Dense, vertical, lived-in
- **Architecture notes:** The most architecturally dense region. Multi-level structures connected by bridges, lifts, and climbing routes. Designed for maximum diversity of body types — narrow passages for small residents, wide cargo routes for large ones, vertical shortcuts for climbers. Street-level, mid-level, and canopy-level are distinct neighborhoods. Warm artificial lighting throughout.

### Nocturnal District

- **Biome:** Low-light environment — caves, deep forest, or perpetually shaded terrain
- **Color palette:** Purple, deep navy, dark with glowing edges
- **Mood:** Moody, intimate, glowing edges
- **Architecture notes:** Designed for residents who are active at night or prefer low light. Bioluminescent or soft artificial lighting — never harsh. Sound-dampened construction. Architecture accounts for echolocation (smooth, reflective surfaces in navigation corridors), night vision (no sudden bright zones), and residents who sleep during the day (blackout construction in living quarters). Intimate scale — smaller gathering spaces, narrower streets.

## World Rules

Hard constraints that must never be violated in any Kelston content.

1. **Architecture adapts to residents, not the other way around.** This is the foundational rule. No resident is forced to use infrastructure not designed for them.

2. **Every region has a distinct visual and emotional mood.** Tundra does not feel like Desert. Rainforest does not feel like Nocturnal District. If a description could belong to any region, it is too generic.

3. **Regions blend at borders — no hard edges between biomes.** A journey from Wetlands to Rainforest passes through increasingly dense vegetation and rising humidity. Transitions are gradual.

4. **Residents are anthro.** Use the term "anthro" specifically. Not "anthropomorphic," not "furry," not "animal people." Anthro.

5. **No one region is "better."** Each has strengths and tradeoffs. The Tundra is not desolate. The Desert is not barren. The Nocturnal District is not sinister. Every region is someone's home.

## Consistency Rules

These are what the **consistency-checker** agent enforces when reviewing content.

### Character Consistency

- A resident assigned to a region stays in that region unless migration is explicitly written into their story.
- A resident's physical description must remain consistent across all mentions.
- A resident's personality and relationships must not contradict earlier entries.

### Regional Consistency

- Color palettes do not bleed across regions. No warm golds in Tundra descriptions. No cool blues dominating Desert scenes.
- Architecture descriptions must match the region's climate and the body types of its residents.
- Lore fragments within a region share that region's established mood.
- A location assigned to a region stays in that region.

### World Consistency

- No modern Earth references. No brand names, no Earth geography, no Earth-specific cultural references.
- No product language. Kelston is a place, not a platform.
- Resident-adapted architecture details must be consistent for the same species or body type across regions (a large resident needs wide corridors everywhere, not just in one location).

### Voice Consistency

- All content follows `brand/voice.md` — atmospheric, sensory, walking pace.
- No CTAs, no urgency words, no startup tone.
- Lore is presented as found objects and fragments, never as wiki entries or encyclopedia articles.
